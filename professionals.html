Diagnosis

Background

The abnormal growth of gestational trophoblast tissue can lead to a range of conditions, from the usually benign partial hydatidiform mole to the rare malignant forms, choriocarcinoma and placental site trophoblastic tumours. Because of the unique biology of these conditions and the specialised approach needed for diagnosis and treatment, trophoblastic disease represents an important area within gynaecology and oncology.

Although rare, the outlook for patients is excellent. With modern care, cure rates exceed 95%, making successful outcomes the norm.

Incidence

In Europe and North America, molar pregnancies occur in 0.2–1.5 per 1,000 live births, although accurate figures are difficult due to variations in diagnostic practices. Rates may be higher in parts of Africa and Asia.

Risk Factors

The risk of a molar pregnancy is highest at the extremes of reproductive age:

  • Slightly increased risk in teenagers (around 1.3-fold)
  • Ten-fold higher risk in women aged 40 and over
  • The risk of complete mole increases more sharply with age than partial mole

Expert review shows that up to 40% of presumed partial moles are actually misclassified and may be complete moles or non-molar tissue.

Molar Evacuation

Suction evacuation is the recommended method for both complete and partial molar pregnancies.

  • Excessive bleeding after evacuation may be managed with a single dose of oxytocin.
  • Repeat evacuations are rarely useful, especially if hCG >1,500 IU/L, and should only be considered after discussion with Charing Cross Hospital (CXH).
  • Audit data shows that second evacuations seldom prevent the need for chemotherapy.

hCG Surveillance

After diagnosis, patients must be registered and monitored with serial hCG tests.

Surveillance Timetable

TypeSchedule
Partial Mole Blood and urine every 2 weeks until hCG is normal, then one repeat urine after 4 weeks completes follow-up.
Complete Mole (normalises ≤8 weeks) Follow-up continues until 6 months post-evacuation.
Complete Mole (normalises >8 weeks) Follow-up continues for 6 months after the first normal hCG result. Monthly urine hCG after normalisation.

Role of hCG in Trophoblastic Disease

hCG is produced by syncytiotrophoblast cells and exists in various molecular forms in malignant disease. It is expressed in nearly all malignant trophoblastic tumours (rare exceptions in PSTT). hCG levels correlate with the number of proliferating cells, guide risk assessment, and monitor treatment response. The natural serum half-life is 24–36 hours, but declines more slowly clinically due to ongoing tumour secretion.

Assay Used for Monitoring: Radioimmunoassay (RIA)

The HMFUS uses a specialised radioimmunoassay (RIA) system for hCG monitoring. The HMFUS is also the national production site for iodinated hCG reagent used by all three UK GTD centres.

Contraception Advice During Monitoring

Hormonal contraception is safe during follow-up and does not increase the risk of invasive mole or choriocarcinoma. Pregnancy should be avoided until all surveillance is complete.

Chemotherapy Indications

HMFUS Indications for Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy is recommended for any of the following:

  • Brain, liver, GI, or lung metastases >2 cm
  • Histological diagnosis of choriocarcinoma
  • Heavy bleeding or intraperitoneal haemorrhage
  • Pulmonary, vulval, or vaginal metastases unless hCG is falling
  • Rising hCG in 2 consecutive serum samples
  • hCG >20,000 IU/L more than 4 weeks after evacuation
  • hCG plateau in 3 consecutive samples
  • Persistently raised hCG at 6 months, even if falling (individualised review)

FIGO Indications for Chemotherapy

  • hCG plateau over 4 values within 3 weeks (±10%)
  • hCG rise >10% across 3 values within 2 weeks
  • Persistence of hCG beyond 6 months post-evacuation

Prognostic Scoring and Treatment Groups

Prognosis is related to hCG level at presentation, presence of metastases, and other risk factors.

Post-Chemotherapy Follow-Up

6-Week Post-Treatment Assessment

Patients are reviewed six weeks after completing chemotherapy to:

  1. Check that all original disease sites have resolved
  2. Repeat Doppler pelvic ultrasound
  3. Repeat chest X-ray or CT/MRI if previously abnormal
  4. Give advice on contraception (avoid pregnancy for 12 months)
  5. Advise on avoiding excessive sun exposure
  6. Discuss relapse risks (2–3% depending on treatment)

All patients continue lifelong hCG marker follow-up.

Risk of Relapse & Long-Term Outlook

Relapse risk is <5% after low-risk treatment and ~3% after EMA-CO. Most relapses occur in the first year, but late recurrences can occur. Even late relapse is usually curable with further therapy. Overall long-term health outlook is excellent.

Fertility After Chemotherapy

  • Most women maintain fertility; periods usually return 2–6 months after treatment
  • Chemotherapy may bring menopause forward slightly (~1 year with methotrexate; ~3 years with EMA-CO)
  • Pregnancy should be avoided for 12 months after treatment
  • 83% of women who wish to conceive achieve at least one live birth
  • No increased risk of foetal abnormalities
  • Risk of another molar pregnancy is 1 in 70

Long-Term Side Effects

Combination chemotherapy slightly increases the long-term risk of second cancers (e.g., myeloid leukaemia, colon, breast, melanoma). Single-agent methotrexate does not increase second cancer risk. This supports early detection and treatment with single-agent therapy wherever possible.

Psychological Impact

Common concerns include the loss of pregnancy, cancer anxiety, treatment effects, delayed fertility, and fear of recurrence. Emotional impacts may persist for years. Counselling and specialist support significantly improve coping and wellbeing — all patients should be made aware of our counselling service.
